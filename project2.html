Electric Bike PCB

As part of the study of circuit design using semiconductor diodes and field-effect transistors, this project aimed to create a voltage doubling circuit simulating a battery powered device. Although the core of this project was to double a DC voltage only an AC voltage supply was allowed. This supply would have to be converted to a DC voltage before doubling. A restriction was also placed on using any kind of pulse generating integrated circuits, such as the 555 timer IC. After designing a half-wave rectifier circuit to convert the AC voltage to a DC voltage, my team and I decided on using a boost converter circuit to achieve the output voltage required. A working boost converter circuit needs a pulse signal to be sent to a transistor. This meant that my we had to make our own pulse generating circuit. After much deliberation and testing, we settled on using a stable NAND gate oscillator circuit to produce the signal needed for the transistor. By calculating the values of the resistors and capacitor, we were able to produce a clocking signal with a duty cycle of 50%. The rectifier, NAND gate oscillator, and boost converter circuits were designed and tested using the simulation software SPICE. After seeing that the circuits worked together as desired, we constructed the physical circuit and ran tests. With no load, the rectifier circuit gave a voltage output of 9.57 volts with less than 0.2% voltage ripple. This 9.57 volt output was then doubled to 19.82 volts with the boost converter circuit.Testing the circuit with loads of 5kÎ© and greater, the output was consistently around 20 volts.
